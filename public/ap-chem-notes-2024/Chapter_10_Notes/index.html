Condensed States of matter: Liquid and solid

The forces that cause a substance to form a liquid or solid may involve covalent or ionic bonding, or they may involve weaker interactions usually called intermolecular forces.

For a substance to exhibit a dipole moment is for it to behave in an electric field as if it has a center of positive charge and a center of negative charge.

Dipole-dipole attraction: when two molecules attract each other electrostatically by lining up so that the positive and negative ends are close to each other (e.g. water’s hydrogen bonds).

Hydrogen bond: An unusually strong type of dipole-dipole attraction between hydrogen and highly electronegative atoms (such as nitrogen, oxygen, or fluorine).

Hydrogen bonds have significant impacts on the physical properties of molecules, often raising the boiling point of the liquid form of a substance substantially (because of the added hydrogen bond forces).

London dispersion forces: the forces that exist among noble gas atoms and nonpolar molecules. Especially in large atoms, electrons—even among molecules considered nonpolar—can “accidentally” sway to one side, causing temporary dipoles, creating attraction forces between

Polarizability: the ease with which the electron “cloud” of an atom can be distorted to give a dipolar charge distribution. Essentially, the likelihood of London dispersion forces forming. As atomic # increases, so does the # of electrons, and with more electrons there is a higher chance of those electrons distorting and forming London dispersion forces. Therefor, as atomic # increases, polarizability increases. This also means that the importance of London dispersion forces increases greatly as the size of the atom increases.

Surface tension: the resistance of a liquid to an increase in its surface area. Liquids with large intermolecular forces tend to have relatively high surface tensions.

Capillary action: the rising of a liquid in a narrow tube, typically exhibited by polar liquids.

Viscosity: a measure of a liquid’s resistance to flow. Viscosity is strongly dependent on intermolecular forces.

Two very broad categories in which we can classify solids are crystalline solids, those with a highly regular arrangement of their components, and

amorphous solids, those with considerable disorder in their structures.

Lattice: a 3d system of points designating the positions of the components of a crystalline solid.

Ionic solids: has ions at the points of the lattice that describe the structure of the solid.

Molecular solids: has discrete covalently bonded molecules at each of its lattice points.

Atomic solids: has simply atoms at each lattice point. Eg. diamond, which is made from entirely crystalline carbon atoms.

Metallic solids: has exclusively metal atoms at lattice points. Positively charged metal ions surrounded by a “sea” of electrons => metallic bonding

Band model / molecular orbital model for metals: a model that gives a more detailed view of the electron energies and motions, compared to the quite simple “electron sea model” which envisions a regular array of metal cations in a “sea” of valence electrons.

Alloy: a substance that contains a mixture of elements and has metallic properties.

Substitutional alloy: some of the host metal atoms are replaced by other metal atoms of similar size (eg. brass, of which about ⅓ is zinc and ⅔ copper).

Interstitial alloy: some of the interstices (holes) in the closest packed metal structure are occupied by small atoms (eg. steel, which has carbon atoms in the holes of an iron crystal).

Network solids: atomic solids (just atoms at each lattice point) that contain strong directional covalent bonds.

Ceramics: nonmetallic materials that are strong, brittle, and resistant to heat, typically made from clays (which contain silicates) and hardened by firing at high temperatures.

Semiconductor: a substance in which some electrons can cross the gap between the filled and empty molecular orbitals at 25 deg C, and whose conductivity increases at higher temperatures.

N-type semiconductor: a substance whose conductivity is increased by doping it with atoms having MORE valence electrons than the atoms in the host crystal. The extra valence electrons lie close in energy to the conduction bands and can be easily excited to conduct electricity.

P-type semiconductor: a substance whose conductivity is increased by doping it with atoms having LESS valence electrons than the atoms in the host crystal. The reduction in valence electrons leaves “holes,” or electron vacancies, which can also act as charge carriers for a semiconductor, increasing conductivity.

Equilibrium vapor pressure: the pressor of the vapor present at equilibrium, when the rate of condensation equals the rate of evaporation.

Sublimation: going directly from solid state to gaseous state (skipping liquid state).

Phase diagram: a convenient way of representing the phases of a substance as a function of temperature and pressure.
