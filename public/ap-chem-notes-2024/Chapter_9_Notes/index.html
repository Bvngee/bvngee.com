Writing Lewis structures to model certain compounds’ bonding is simple and works great. But when we start to take into account the specific orbitals of each of a compound’s atoms, things can get to be more tricky. For example, in Methane (CH4), the hydrogens each have one 1s orbital, and the carbon has three 2p orbitals and one 2s orbital (remember: only the outer shell is considered for bonding). That seems to show that there will be two different types of C–H bonds, but from experiment we know there aren’t. As such, we can determine that carbon’s different orbitals mix to form four identical orbitals for bonding; called sp3 orbitals.

This is called Hybridization: The mixing of the native atomic orbitals to form special orbitals for bonding. In the case of carbon, we say that it is “sp3 hybridized.”

Whenever a set of equivalent tetrahedral atomic orbitals is required by an atom, this model assumes that the atom adopts a set of sp3 orbitals; it is sp3 hybridized.

sp3 isn’t the only form of hybridization. In Ethylene (C2H4), for example, each Carbon atom has two hydrogen atoms and one other carbon atom (double) bonded to it. This requires a trigonal planar arrangement (120 degree angles). Since carbon’s one 2s and three 2p orbitals do not fit that, we need a new set of hybrid orbitals. (somewhat arbitrarily) three sp2 hybrid orbitals are used (and the remaining unused 2p orbital from carbon is left behind, oriented perpendicular to the trigonal plane).

The bonds in ethylene can be described using those hybrid and non-hybrid orbitals. Each of the sp2 orbitals is used to share electrons (with H and with the other C). Those electrons occupy the space in the very center between the two atoms, called a Sigma Bond(σ). Additionally, electrons can occupy the space between the two “vertical” P orbitals on each C atom, called Pi Bonds(π). In summary, Sigma bonds form from orbitals whose lobes point toward each other, and Pi bonds result from parallel orbitals. A double bond always consists of one sigma bond and one pi bond.

Whenever an atom is surrounded by three effective pairs, A set of sp2 hybrid orbitals is required.

In a case like CO2—two effective pairs, both double bonds—we again need another hybrid orbital, as neither sp2 nor sp3 fit the characteristics of two pairs and linear at 180 degrees. To obtain this using carbon’s three 2p orbitals and one 2s orbital, we use two hybrid sp orbitals, leaving two 2p orbitals behind. The two sp orbitals form sigma bonds with one oxygen each, and the two leftover 2p orbitals form pi bonds with one oxygen each (double bond is always 1 sigma + 1 pi).

Two effective pairs around an atom will always require sp hybridization of that atom.

To account for atoms that don’t obey the quartet rule in a molecule (>4 bonds/effective pairs), like PCl5, we use dsp3 hybridization: one d orbital, one s orbital and three p orbitals. For 6 effective pairs, like in SF6, we use d2sp3 hybridization. Since each of the Cl’s in PCl5 and each of the F’s in SF6 all have 4 effective pairs (one bond + 3 lone electron pairs), they are assumed to use sp3 hybridization.

Molecular Orbital Model: Another model used to describe bonding that has advantages over the localized electron model.

Just as atomic orbitals are solutions to the quantum mechanical treatment of atoms, molecular orbitals (MOs) are solutions to the molecular problem.

When analyzing H2 using this model, solving its quantum mechanical equations results in two molecular orbits, MO1 and MO2. MO1 = 1sA + 1sB, and MO2 = 1sA - 1sB (1sA and 1sB are the two 1s orbitals on each H, from the localized electron model)

For H2, the electron probability of both molecular orbitals is centered on the line through the two nuclei; as such, they are called Sigma Molecular Orbitals.

The molecular orbitals of a compound replace the atomic orbitals of its individual atoms.

In the case of hydrogen, the MO1 orbital is lower in energy than the two 1s orbitals of the free H’s, and the MO2 orbital is higher in energy. Since the MO1 orbital is available, and because nature tends towards lower energy, this makes H2 a favorable for formation. This situation is called probonding. If, however, the electrons were forced to occupy the higher-energy MO2 orbital, they would be antibonding (it would be lower energy for them to stay separated).

A bonding molecular orbital is lower in energy than the atomic orbitals that make it up.

An antibonding molecular orbital is higher in energy than the atomic orbitals that make it up.

The labels on molecular orbitals indicate their symmetry (shape), the parent atomic orbitals, and whether they’re bonding or antibonding. * = antibonding. In the case of H2, both MO’s have sigma symmetry (electron probability between nuclei), and both are constructed from 1s atomic orbitals. So the MO’s are labeled as MO1 = σ1s and MO2 = σ1s* .

Molecular electron configurations are written in the same way as for atomic orbitals. Since both electrons occupy the lower energy bonding molecular orbital, σ1s, it is written as σ1s2 .

The number of molecular orbitals will always be the same as the number of atomic orbitals used to construct them.

Bond order: a concept used to indicate bond strength. (larger value = greater bond strength)

Bond order = (# of bonding e-  -  # of antibonding e-) / 2

In H2, the bond order is (2 - 0)/2 = 1. In H2-, with 3 electrons, the bond order is (2 - 1)/2 = ½.

To participate in molecular orbitals, atomic orbitals must overlap in space. (Valence orbitals only)

Electron probability that lies above and/or below the line between the nuclei => Pi Molecular Orbitals.

Paramagnetism causes a substance to be attracted into an including magnetic field.

Diamagnetism causes a substance to be repelled from an including magnetic field.

Paramagnetism is associated with unpaired electrons and diamagnetism is associated with paired electrons.

While there are correlations between the bond order, bond length and bond energy within the molecular orbital model, it does not give us exact values (eg. two different bond order 1 bonds can still be very different energy).

Since every double bond has one sigma and one pi bond (and every bonder order higher has one sigma bond), we can assume that the non-localization of orbitals is really only specific to pi bonds—the sigma bonds are localized but the pi bonds are not. This is useful for drawing better orbital diagrams; it essentially allows us to combine the Localized Electron Model and the Molecular Orbital Model.

Consider C6H6 , which has 6 carbons each bonded with one sigma bond, and where every other bond also has a pi bond (via the leftover p orbitals perpendicular to the molecule’s plane).
