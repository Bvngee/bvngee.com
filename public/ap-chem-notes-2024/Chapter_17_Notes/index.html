Chapter 17 Notes - Spontaneity, Entropy, and Free Energy

A process is said to be spontaneous if it occurs without outside intervention. Spontaneous processes may be fast or slow.

Entropy (denoted by the symbol S): although there is no simple definition, it can be viewed as a measure of molecular randomness or disorder. The driving force for a spontaneous process is an increase in the entropy of the universe.

Second law of thermodynamics: In any spontaneous process there is always an increase in the entropy of the universe.   ΔSuniv = ΔSsys + ΔSsurr   It is the sign of ΔSuniv that tells us whether or not a process is spontaneous. If ΔSsurr is negative and overpowers the positive ΔSsys , ΔSuniv will also be negative and thus the process will not be favorable. How do we know which one will overpower and control the situation? It depends on temperature! With the example of evaporation of water, the process is only favorable / spontaneous / ΔSuniv > 0  at temperatures above 100 degrees C. Otherwise, condensation is the favorable reaction.

The entropy changes in the surroundings are primarily determined by heat flow.

The impact of the transfer of a given quantity of heat energy to or from the surroundings will be greater at lower temperatures (i.e. giving $50 to someone broke vs a millionaire).

The sign of ΔSsurr depends on the direction of heat flow (exothermic process in system => surroundings gain heat => increase ΔSsurr)

The magnitude of ΔSsurr depends directly on the quantity of heat transferred and inversely on temperature.

Exothermic process:

ΔSsurr = quantity of heat (J) / temperature (K)

Endothermic process:

ΔSsurr = - quantity of heat (J) / temperature (K)

Since heat flow at constant pressure = change in enthalpy (ΔH),

ΔSsurr = - ΔH / T

Gibbs free energy: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, where H is enthalpy, T is temperature (kelvin), and S is entropy. At constant temperature and pressure, ΔSuniv = - ΔG / T . Thus a process (at constant T and P) is spontaneous when ΔG is negative (direction of decreasing free energy).

Third law of thermodynamics: the entropy of a perfect crystal at 0K is zero.

ΔS°reaction = ∑npS°products - ∑nrS°reactants

Standard free energy change: the change in free energy that will occur if the reactants in their standard states are converted to the products in their standard states.

Standard free energy of formation (ΔG°f): the change in free energy that accompanies the formation of 1 mole of that substance from its constituent elements with all reactants and products in their standard states.

S large volume > S small volume

S low pressure > S high pressure

A chemical system will always seek the lowest possible free energy, which is the equilibrium position.

The maximum possible useful work obtainable from a process at constant T and P is equal to the change in free energy: wmax = ΔG . IRL, obtaining all this energy is not possible; some will always be wasted (thus the second law of thermodynamics).

First law of thermodynamics: You can’t win, you can only break even.

Second law of thermodynamics: You can’t break even.

ΔG° = -RTlnK , where K is the equilibrium constant.
