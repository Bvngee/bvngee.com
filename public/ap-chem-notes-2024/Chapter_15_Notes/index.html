Chapter 15 Notes - Acid-Base Equilibria

The six (common) strong acids (assume the rest are weak):

HCl, HBr, HI, HNO3, H2SO4, HClO4

The six (common) strong bases (assume the rest are weak):

LiOH, NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)2, Sr(OH)2, Ba(OH)2

Buffer Problems

A buffer is a solution of a weak acid or base and its salt (which is its conjugate).

A buffer resists a change in pH.

How it works: since a buffer consists of both an acid or base and its conjugate, an acid and a base are present in all buffer solutions. Thus if a little strong acid is added to the buffer, there is already a base complement ready and waiting to neutralize the “invader,” reacting together to form salt (which was already present in the solution). Same goes for some base; if a small amount of strong base is added to a buffer, the acid reacts with it to form salt (and water).

Remember:

  • Ka * Kb = Kw = [H+][OH-]   (Kw = 1*1014 only at 25C!!)
  • pH = -log[H+] ,  pOH = -log[OH-] ,  pH + pOH = 14
  • Any titration involving a weak A/B is a buffer problem.

The one and only buffer equation:
[H+] = Ka 

If acid is added to the buffer, add the moles (convert everything to moles, not molarity) of acid to the numerator and subtract it from the denominator (think how a buffer works: the base is self-sacrificing to neutralize the incoming acid). If base is added to the buffer, do the reverse: add the moles of base to the denominator and subtract it from the numerator.

Note: When the concentration of weak acid equals the concentration of weak base,

[H+] = Ka  and thus pH = pKa = -log(Ka) ! This is useful when asked to construct a buffer of a specific pH given a table of Ka’s; choose a Ka with an exponent close to the desired pH and use equal concentrations of the acid and base.

When the concentrations of acid and base in a buffer are equal, it is a perfect buffer; that is, it has equal ability to neutralize invader base and invader acid. When asked “which system works best” in buffer creation problems, the one with the [WA]/[WB] ratio closest to one is the one that works best.

Acid / Base Titration Curves

Just look for the geometric midpoint of the straight vertical bit - that’s the equivalence point.

A weak acid will have a pH level that starts higher and usually with an initial bump:

If the acid AND the base are BOTH strong, the equivalence point pH will be 7. Otherwise, you have to do an equilibrium problem. In reality, it’s just a stoichiometry problem with a limiting reactant (the excess is responsible for the pH).

To know if the pH at the equivalence point will be greater or less than 7, just look at which is stronger:

strong acid + weak base => pH < 7

weak acid + strong base => pH > 7

strong acid + strong base => pH = 7

Equivalence point =/= end point:

The end point is when the indicator changes color.

The equivalence point is when the # of moles of acid equals the # of moles of base.

If you’ve made a careful choice, they’ll be achieved at the same time.

Things to Note on Titration Curves

  • Weak acids have a little bump initially, strong acids don’t
  • pH at the midpoint of the titration (the ½ equivalence point). Since halfway to the equivalence point ½ of the acid/base has been neutralized, [WA]/[WB] = 1, thus [H+] = kA and pH = pKa.
  • pH at the equivalence point. It just represents the pH of the salt, as all of the acid/base has been neutralized (into salt and water). Use #moles of salt / VT to get Msalt, use that as the initial in the RICE table.
  • pH after the equivalence point. Now pH is > 7; you can calculate it using

pH = 14 - pOH if needed.

Hydrolysis: essentially the reverse of a neutralization reaction. Written in net ionic form.

  • If a strong base is reacted with a weak acid, “Strong wins!” and the salt is basic, thus OH- forms as a product.
  • If a strong acid is reacted with a weak base, “Strong wins!” awins!” and the salt is acidic, thus H+ forms as a product.
  • The “strong” portion of the acid or base is the source of the “spectator” ion (does NOT react with the water).
  • The remaining ion (or neutral molecule) is the reactant that reacts with the water.

Steps the write the hydrolysis reaction (using NaCN salt as an example):

  1. Which acid? Which base? (HCN, NaOH)
  2. Is the acid strong or weak? (HCN, weak) Is the base strong or weak? (NaOH, strong) “Strong wins!” (salt is basic, OH- forms as product) “Strong is the spectator” (Na+ spectates, CN- reacts with the water)
  3. CN- + HOH -> OH- + HCN   Done! (HOH is just H2O)
  4. In an acid/base titration where either participant is weak, this final equation is what you use in the R part of a RICE table to solve for pH / pOH. (note: at eq.pnt., moles acid = moles base = moles salt)

Indicators are weak acids. Important: an indicator’s useful range = pKa ± 1  (i.e. for an indicator with Ka = 1.0 * 10^-5, the useful range is about 4 to 6.
