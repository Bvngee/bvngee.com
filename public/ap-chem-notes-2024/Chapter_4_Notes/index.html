Chapter 4 Notes

Aqueous Solutions: solutions in which water is the dissolving medium, or solvent.

Water is one of the most important substances on earth.

Water is a polar molecule; it has an unequal distribution of charge. That is, the electrons shared between the H and O atoms spend more time around the O, which leads to a slightly negative charge around the O and a slightly positive charge around the H’s.

Hydration: The process by which ionic solids are dissolved in water. The slightly positive charge of the H atoms in the water molecules attract to the negative anions, and the slightly negative charge of the O atoms in the water attract to the positive cations.

Hydrated ions, or dissolved ions, are dispersed (move around independently).

The solubility of ionic substances in water varies greatly. But typically, it depends on the attraction of the substance’s ions to each other (forces holding the solid together), and their attraction to water.

Water also dissolves many nonionic substances, depending on their molecular structures (e.g. ethanol, which contains a similar polar O–H bond and is found in beer/wine/mixed drinks). But substances that have no polar bonds and are not ionic typically won’t dissolve in water (e.g. animal fat). Like dissolves like  is a good generalization for predicting solubility.

Solution = homogeneous mixture = the same throughout (e.g. first and last sip of coffee taste the same)

Substances that are strong electrical conductors contain strong electrolytes, whereas substances that are weak electrical conductors contain weak electrolytes.

Substances that do not conduct electricity at all contain nonelectrolytes.

The conductivity of dissolved substances depends on how ionized they are when dissolved.

An acid is a substance that produces H+ ions (protons) when dissolved in water.

Strong acid = strong electrolytes, Weak acid = weak electrolytes.

Base = soluble ionic compound that produces the hydroxide ion (OH-) when dissolved in water.

  • Sulfuric acid, nitric acid, and hydrochloric acid are aqueous solutions and should be written in chemical equations as H2SO4(aq), HNO3(aq), and HCl(aq), respectively.
  • A strong acid completely dissociates into its ions. Ex. if 100 molecules of HCl are dissolved in water, 100 H+ ions and 100 Cl- ions are produced.
  • Sulfuric acid is special, because it produces 2 H+ ions but only one is completely dissociated.

Molarity (M) = concentration (of solute in solution) = moles of solute per volume of solution in liters (moles of solute / liters of solution)

A solution that is 1.0 M contains one mole of solute per liter of solution.

Types of chemical reactions:

  • Precipitation reactions
  • Two solutions are mixed, and an insoluble substance forms which becomes a solid and separates from the solution (the precipitate).
  • Three types of equations can be used to describe them:
  • Formula equation (shows full compounds and assumes separation into ions for reaction to take place)
  • Complete ionic equation (splits the compound into individual ions)
  • Net ionic equation (above, but removes all the ions that are not part of the precipitate in the end (called spectator ions))
  • Acid-base reactions
  • Often called neutralization reactions
  • when just enough base is added to react with the acid in a solution, the acid is “neutralized”
  • Oxidation-reduction reactions
  • (also redox reactions)
  • Reactions in which electrons are transferred. None of the products or reactants are ionic, yet electrons are still transferred.

More general definition of acid and base:

Acid = proton donor.

Base = proton acceptor.

(when dissolved in water)

The hydroxide (OH-) ion is such a strong base that for purposes of stoichiometric calculations it can be assumed to react completely with any weak acid that we will encounter.

Volumetric Analysis is a technique for determining the amount of a certain substance by doing a titration.

Titration = delivery of a titrant (measured volume of a solution of known concentration) from a buret into the solution containing the substance being analyzed (analyte).

Equivalence Point = the point in titration where exactly enough titrant has been added to react with the analyte.

Endpoint = the point where the indicator actually changes color. The goal is to make this point as close to the equivalence point as possible.

Standardizing a solution = finding its concentration.

Oxidation States (or oxidation number) keep track of electrons in oxidation-reduction reactions, particularly involving covalent substances.

The oxidation states of atoms in covalent compounds represent the charges the atoms would have if all of the shared electrons were assigned completely to the atom that has the stronger attraction for electrons, or in compounds with identical atoms, split equally.

Non-integer oxidation states are possible (albeit rare), because they simply attempt to keep track of shared electrons. For example, in Fe3O4, the O has an oxidation state of -8, meaning the three Fe atoms must have a charge of +8 to balance out the total charge in the compound. Thus their charges each are +⅜.

Oxidation = increase in oxidation state (loss of electrons).

Reduction = decrease in oxidation state (gain of electrons).

To balance an oxidation-reduction reaction, first assign oxidation states to all the atoms in the unbalanced equation. Determine which atoms’ oxidation states increase or decrease in the reaction, and use coefficients to equalize the electrons gained and lost (these reactions are about transferring electrons). Then balance the rest by eye.
