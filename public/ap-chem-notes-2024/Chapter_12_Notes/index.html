Chapter 12 Notes - Chemical Kinetics

Chemical kinetics: The area of chemistry that deals with the rates of reactions.

The reaction rate of a chemical reaction is defined as the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time:

rate = (concentration of A at time t2 - concentration of A at time t1) / (t2 - t1)

rate = Δ[A] / Δt

Rate law: an expression that shows how the rate of a reaction depends on the concentrations of reactants, such as: rate = k[NO2]n for the decomposition of nitrogen dioxide, where k is a proportionality constant called the rate constant, and n is called the order of the reactant (solely determined by experiment).

Note: After a given reaction takes place for a long time (in a closed container), the reverse reaction starts to become important, causing the decomposition of NO2 to depend on the difference between the forward and reverse reactions. The reason the concentrations of the products do not show up as a factor in NO2’s decomposition rate law is because the rate is being studied under conditions where the reverse reaction is insignificant (does not contribute).

It is important to be explicit about which products/reactants are used to define a reaction’s rate law; in the example of the decomposition of NO2, the two different products are produced at different rates (two NO’s per O2).

- Δ[NO2] / Δt  =  Δ[NO] / Δt  =  2 * Δ[O2] / Δt

therefore k[NO2]n can be defined in multiple ways.

The above rate law for the decomposition of NO2 (k[NO2]n) shows how the rate of the reaction depends on the concentration of NO2. There is another type of rate law however, which shows how the concentrations depend on time.

Differential rate law (also just called rate law): rate / concentration

Integrated rate law: concentration / time

Which one is used often depends simply on which data is easier to collect. Once one is determined though, the other is known.

Integrated first-order rate law: the integrated rate law for a generic chemical reaction with an order of 1. It is derived from the differential rate law (Δ[A] / Δt) using integration:

ln[A] = -kt + ln[A]0 , where [A]0 is the concentration of reactant A at time t=0.

For chemical equations with an order of 1, the integrated first-order rate law will always be of the form y = mx + b (y=ln[A], x=t, m=-k, b=ln[A]0), and thus a straight line.

Half life of a reactant: the time required for the reactant to reach half its original concentration, designated by the symbol t1/2 .

The integrated first-order rate law can also be expressed as a ratio of [A] and [A]0 , which is especially useful when solving half-life problems.

ln[A] = -kt + ln[A]0  ->  ln([A]0/[A]) = kt , and since at t1/2 , [A] = ½[A]0 , ln(2) = kt1/2.

For second-order rate laws:

Differential rate law: rate = -Δ[A] / Δt = k[A]2

Integrated rate law: 1 / [A] = kt + 1 / [A]0

Plotting 1 / [A] versus t will produce a straight line with slope equal to k.

The similar half-life equation for the second-order integrated rate law is kt1/2 = 1 / [A]0 .

For a second-order reaction, each successive half-life is double the preceding one.

For zero-order rate laws:

Differential rate law: rate = -Δ[A] / Δt = k[A]0 = k

Integrated rate law: [A] = -kt + [A]0

Plotting [A] versus t will produce a straight line with slope equal to -k.

The similar half-life equation for the zero-order integrated rate law is kt1/2 = ½[A]0 .

For reactions with large numbers of reactants, experiments can be conducted in environments where all but one reactant is at extremely high concentrations, which effectively makes [A] = [A]0 . Because of this, a high order reaction can be treated as a first-order reaction for each reactant. The rate law for each isolated reactant is called a pseudo-first-order rate law.

Reaction mechanism: a series of elementary steps that satisfy the following:

  1. The sum of the elementary steps must give the overall balanced equation
  2. The mechanism must agree with the experimentally determined rate law

Elementary step: each of the individual reaction “steps.” Elementary steps’ rate laws are able to be written from their molecularity.

Molecularity: the number of species that must collide to produce the reaction indicated by a step.

In many reactions that take place in more than one step, certain species will be produced by one step and then immediately consumed by the next; these are neither products nor reactants, and are called intermediates.

Unimolecular step: a reaction involving one molecule.

Bimolecular step: a reaction involving the collision of two species.

Trimolecular step: ..you get the idea

Chemical reactions speed up when the temperature is increased.
