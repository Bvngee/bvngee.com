Chapter 16 Notes - Solubility and Complex Ion Equilibria

Solubility product constant / solubility product: An equilibrium constant for the dissolving of soluble solids. Ksp = [A+]a[B-]b where a and b are the relative concentrations (coefficients) of the cation and anion. Written according to the law of mass action.

Solubility is an equilibrium position (can be multiple at a given temp); solubility product (Ksp) is an equilibrium constant (only one per reaction at a given temp).

(x is solubility)

We must be careful using Ksp values to predict the relative solubility of a group of salts. If the salts produce the same number of ions, then the largest Ksp will be the most soluble. However if the salts produce different numbers of ions, then the Ksp values cannot be compared directly to determine the order of solubility.

For an example of a salt dissolving in water that contains an ion common in the salt, take solid silver chromate (Ag2CrO4) in a 0.1M solution of AgNO3 . Since NO3- is not found in the salt, it can be ignored. Relevant initial concentrations:

[Ag+]0 = 0.1M, [CrO42-]0 = 0 . The system comes to equilibrium according to the reaction

Ag2CrO4(s) <-> 2Ag+(aq) + CrO42-(aq) , and Ksp = [Ag+]2[CrO42-] = 9*10^-12 is given.

Apply a variable x denoting change to equilibrium:

[Ag+] = [Ag+]0 + change = 0.1 + 2x

[CrO42-] = [CrO42-]0 + change = 0 + x

Using the Ksp formula, 9*10^-12 = (0.1 + 2x)2(x) . This looks complicated, but since x is expected to be way smaller than 0.1 (Ksp is small => eq.pt. Is far to the left => change is small), we can simplify to (0.1)2(x). Thus, x = 9*10^-12 / 0.01 = 9*10^-10M

Plugging X in, we get

[Ag+] = 0.1 + 2x = 0.1 M

[CrO42-] = 0 + x = 9*10^-10 M

Since [CrO42-] come entirely from the dissolved salt, we know that the solubility of AgCrO4 in 1.0M AgNO3 is 9*10^-10 M.

This is much less than its solubility in pure water, which is an example of the common ion effect. The solubility of a solid is lowered if the solution already contains ions common to the solid.

The pH of a solution can greatly affect a salt’s solubility. IF the salt’s anion is from a weak acid (strong base), its solubility will be greater in acidic solutions, as the H+ ions will react with the strong base, pushing the equilibrium point to the right (common ion effect). For example, the PO43- in Ag3PO4 would react with H+ ions in an acidic solution to form HPO32-, reducing the concentration of PO43- and thus pushing the eq.pt. to the right.

Ion product: defined just like the expression for Ksp for a given solid except that initial concentrations are used instead of equilibrium concentrations. I.e. for CaF2,

Q = [Ca2+]0[F2-]0

The ion product is used to predict whether a solid precipitate will form when two solutions are mixed, as follows:

  • If Q > Ksp , precipitation occurs until the point where the concentrations are reduced enough to satisfy Ksp .

  • If Q < Ksp , no precipitation occurs.

Sometimes we want to know not just whether a precipitate will form but also the equilibrium concentrations after it has occurred. Taking Pb(NO3)2 + NaI as an example which makes PbI2 (Q > Ksp => precipitation will occur), since Ksp is quite small, most of the ions when mixed will exist as PbI2 . I.e. the reaction Pb2+ + 2I- -> PbI2(s) goes nearly to completion.

Note: If, when two solutions are mixed, their reaction goes virtually to completion, it is essential to do the stoichiometry calculations before considering the equilibrium calculations. So in the case of PbI2 , we will first let the precipitation go 100% to completion, and then adjust it back to meet equilibrium.

Pb2+ + 2I- -> PbI2 (doesn't matter here)

Before Rxn 5 mmol 20 mmol

After Rxn 0 mmol 20-2(5)=10 mmol I-

Then,

[Pb2+]0 = 0 , [I-]0 = 10 mmol / 300ml = 3.33*10^-2 M

Now the reaction is, PbI2(s) <-> Pb2+(aq) + 2I-(aq) ; i.e., how much PbI2 will dissolve in a 3.33*10^-2 M solution of NaI?

Plugging everything into Ksp = 1.4 * 10^-8 (given) = [Pb2+][I-]2 = (x)(3.33e-2+2x)^2

Simplifying, 1.4e-8 = x(3.33e-2)^2, x = 1.3*10^-5

[Pb2+]0 = x = 1.3*10^-5 , [I-]0 = 3.33*10^-2 + 2x = 3.33*10^-2

Complex ion: a charged species consisting of a metal ion surrounded by ligands.

Ligand: a Lewis base; a molecule or ion having a lone electron pair that can be donated to an empty orbital on the metal ion to form a covalent bond (common ligands: H2O, NH3, Cl-, CN-)

The number of ligands attached to a metal ion is called the coordination number. The most common coordination numbers are 6, 4, and 2 (here’s a 6 example: Co(H2O)62+)

In stepwise reactions, the overall equilibrium constant is the sum of the equilibrium expressions for each step’s reaction.
