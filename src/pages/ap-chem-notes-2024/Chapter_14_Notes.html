Arrhenius concept of acids and bases: a limited and early model suggesting that acids produce hydrogen ions in aqueous solutions, while bases produce hydroxide ions. Only works for aqueous solutions and one kind of base (the hydroxide ion).

Bronstedâ€“Lowry model of acids and bases: a more general model proposing that an acid is a proton (H+) donor, and a base is a proton acceptor.

This equation really represents a competition between two bases (H2O and A-) for the proton. The equilibrium position (i.e. how much of the dissolved acid will be in the form HA vs A-) depends on which base has a higher affinity for H+.

Ka = [H3O+][A-] / [HA] = [H+][A-] / [HA] , where Ka is called the acid dissociation constant. The concentration of water is excluded from the reactants in the same way that pure solids/liquids are omitted from the equilibrium expression - we can assume that in a dilute solution, the concentration of water remains essentially constant when an acid is dissolved.

The strength of an acid is defined by the equilibrium position of its dissociation (ionization) reaction: HA(aq) + H2O(l) <â€“> H3O+(aq) + A- (aq) .

A strong acid is one whose equilibrium lies far to the right (most A is in A- form).

A weak acid is one whose equilibrium lies far to the left (most A is in HA form).

A strong acid yields a weak conjugate base (one with a low affinity for H+). In other words, for strong acids, water is a much stronger base than the acidâ€™s conjugate base (A-), so the acid will dissociate mostly to A- giving the proton up to water. Conversely,

Monoprotic acid: an acid having one acidic proton.

[ Diprotic / Polyprotic ] acid: an acid having [ 2 / 2+ ] acidic protons.

Oxyacids: acids in which the acidic proton is attached to an oxygen atom. Many common acids are oxyacids.

A substance is said to be amphoteric if it can behave either as an acid or as a base. Water is the most common amphoteric substance, as one water molecule can donate a proton to another (becoming OH- and H3O+) (called the autoionization of water).

pH scale: a convenient way to represent solution acidity. Since [H+] is usually quite small, we use a log in base 10: pH = â€“log[H+] . Thus, a solution with a pH of 3 has a 10 times higher concentration of H+ ions than a solution with a pH of 4.

When calculating the pH of a weak acid solution, the first step is always to figure out which are the major species in the solution. For example, in a highly acidic solution, [OH-] will be very small and thus is a minor species. Then, decide which of the major species can furnish H+ ions (eg. H2O <-> H+ + OH- , or HF <-> H+ + F-). In aqueous solutions, typically one source of H+ can be singled out as dominant. Then, use the same RICE process as in chapter 13 when calculating equilibrium concentrations; use the known initial concentrations and Ka value to solve for an unknown variable x representing the change in concentration to equilibrium. Percent dissociation: a way to measure the amount of weak acid that has dissociated in achieving equilibrium in an aqueous solution.

Percent dissociated = (amount dissociated (mol/L) / initial concentration (mol/L)) * 100%

In the same sense as strong acids, strong bases are bases that dissociate completely when dissolved in an aqueous solution.

The general reaction between a base B and water is given by:

B(aq) + H2O(l) <â€“> BH+(aq) + OH-(aq)

(base) (acid) (conjugate acid) (conjugate base)

The equilibrium constant for this general reaction is:

Kb = [BH+][OH-] / [B] , where Kb is called the base dissociation constant and always refers to the reaction of a base with water to form a conjugate acid and the hydroxide ion.

Bases (B in the above reaction) compete with OH-, a very strong base, for the H+ ion.

Bases with low Kb values are weak bases, as the H+ ions mostly stay with water.

Ka < 1*100 (neg pKa) means weak acid (small amount has dissolved into A- and H+);

Ka > 1*100 (pos pKa) means strong acid (much has dissolved).

Kb < ~0.1 (1*10-1) (high pKb) means weak base (small amount of base has accepted protons);

Kb > ~0.1 (1*10-1) (low pKb) means strong base (much has become BH)

pKa = -log(Ka), pKb = -log(Kb) (eg. Ka = 1*103, pKa = -3, 1*10-4, pKa = 4)

Salt: simply another name for ionic compound.

Salts that consist of the cations of strong bases and the anions of strong acids have no effect on [H+] when dissolved in water. Thus, aqueous solutions of KCl, NaCl, NaNO3, and KNO3 are neutral (pH = 7).

For any weak acid and its conjugate base, Ka * Kb = Kw .

For any salt whose cation has neutral properties (such as Na+ or K+), and whose anion is the conjugate base of a weak acid, the aqueous solution will be basic.

In general, salts in which the anion is not a base and the cation is the conjugate acid of a weak base produce acidic solutions.

Lewis model for acids and bases: an acid is an electron-pair acceptor, and a base is an electron-pair donor.
