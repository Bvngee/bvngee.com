Chapter 8 Notes (Bonding - General Concepts)

Bond energy: the energy required to break a bond.

The energy of interaction between a pair of ions can be calculated using Coulomb's Law, in the form: E = (2.31 * 10^-19 J*nm) * (Q1Q2/r), where r is the distance between the ion centers in nanometers, and Q1 and Q2 are the numerical ion charges. It can also be used to calculate the repulsive energy when two like-charged ions are brought together.

The distance between two atoms where energy is minimal is called the Bond Length.

Electrons shared between identical atoms = covalent bond. Electrons transferred to form two ions = ionic bond. But in the middle are cases where not-so-different atoms share electrons unequally, which are polar covalent bonds. For example, H-F .

Electronegativity: The ability of an atom in a molecule to attract shared electrons to itself.

Considering a hypothetical molecule, HX, the relative electronegativity of the two atoms (H and X) is calculated by comparing their measured bond energy with their “expected” bond energy (expected is found by H-H bond energy + X-X bond energy / 2).

Δ = (H-X)act - (H-X)exp . If H and X have identical electronegativities, the actual and expected bond energies will be the same and thus Δ = 0. But if X has a greater electronegativity than H, electrons will spend more time around the X atom, so it will have a slightly negative charge (thus Δ will not = 0). (such a bond can be viewed as ionic and covalent.) The attraction between the partially and oppositely charged atoms leads to a greater bond strength, so (H-X)act will be the greater value.

A molecule (such as HF) that has a center of positive charge and a center of negative charge is said to be dipolar, or to have a dipole moment.

Some molecules have polar bonds but do not have a dipole moment; this happens because its bond polarities are aligned in such a way that they cancel each other out (for example, in CO2, as both O atoms are on opposite sides of the C atom).

lattice energy: the change in energy that takes place when separated gaseous ions are packed together to form an ionic solid. This indicates how strongly the two ions attract each other in an ionic compound in the solid state. Often referred to as the amount of energy released when an ionic solid forms from its ions. As this process is exothermic, the sign of lattice energy is negative.

Consider a hypothetical in which two ions form an ionic solid:

M+(s) + X-(g) -> MX(s)

To find the lattice energy, we utilize the fact that energy is a state function and break the process into steps. Each step corresponds to some amount of energy, which together can be used to find the lattice energy.

  1. Sublimation (taking from solid state to gas state) of M

  2. Ionization of M (taking away 1 electron to make into

  3. Formation of X- ions from X - corresponds to X’s electron affinity

A more complex example might involve the dissociation of diatomic gas molecules into singular atoms, for example.

Lattice energy can also be represented by a modified form of Coulomb’s Law,

E = k * (Q1Q2/r), where k is a proportionality constant that depends on the solid’s structure and ions’ electron configurations, and again Q1 and Q2 are the charges of the ions. E will have a negative sign with two oppositely charged ions, as their bonding is exothermic (arrives at a lesser-energy state).

Since there is really no good way to classify a molecule as having a strictly ionic or polar bond, the adopted definition for an ionic compound is: any compound that when melted conducts electricity will be classified as ionic.

As the number of shared electron pairs in a bond increases, the bond length shortens.

Bond energies can be used to calculate approximate energies for reactions. Take, for example, H2 + F2 -> 2HF . This involves breaking two bonds (endothermic), H-H and F-F, and forming two bonds (exothermic), both H-F . The total enthalpy change of these processes can be written as ΔH = ∑n * D (bonds broken) - ∑n * D (bonds formed), where D represents the bond energy per mole of bonds (D is always positive), and n represents the moles of a particular type of bond. In English, the change in enthalpy of the reaction is the total (absorbed) energy of all bonds broken minus the total (released) energy of all bonds formed. For our example, ΔH = DH-H + DF-F - 2DH-F .

Localized Electron (LE) Model: a simple model used to describe covalent bonds that assumes that a molecule is composed of atoms that are bound together by sharing pairs of electrons using the atomic orbitals of the bound atoms. Electron pairs in the molecule are assumed to be localized on a particular atom OR in the space between two atoms (their locations are never this simple in real life). Electron pairs localized on an atom are called lone pairs, and those localized between two atoms are called bonding pairs.

The Lewis structure of a molecule shows how the valence electrons are arranged among the atoms in the molecule.

From experiment, chemists have learned that the most important requirement for the formation of a stable compound is that the atoms achieve noble gas configurations.

Resonance is invoked when multiple valid Lewis structures exist for a given molecule AND that molecule has at least one double bond. It is assumed that the molecule’s correct description is not given by any of the valid structures but rather a superposition of all of them. The situation is usually represented by double-headed arrows between all valid structures.

Since the localized electron model is based on electron pairs, it does not work with any molecules that have an off number of electrons (NO, NO2) and another model is needed.

The formal charge of an atom in a molecule is the difference between the number of valence electrons on the free atom and the number of valence electrons assigned to the atom in the molecule. It represents a prediction of atoms’ charges within the molecule, similar to oxidation states (but in practice produces better, less exaggerated results).

Atom’s Formal Charge = (# of valence e-’s on free atom) - (# of valence e-’s assigned to the atom in the molecule)

To do so, we assign the valence electrons in the molecule to the various atoms making the following assumptions:

  1. Lone pair electrons belong entirely to the atom in question

  2. Shared electrons are divided equally between the two sharing atoms

Atoms’ formal charges in molecules are useful as we can make the following two fundamental assumptions:

  1. Atoms in molecules try to have formal charges as close to 0 as possible

  2. Any negative formal charges are expected to reside on the most electronegative atoms

Valence Shell Electron-Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) model: A model used to predict approximate molecular structure by minimizing electron-pair repulsion.

Steps to apply:

  1. Draw the Lewis structure of the molecule

  2. Count the electron pairs and arrange them in the way that minimizes repulsion

  3. Determine the positions of the atoms from the way the electron pairs are shared.

  4. Determine the name of the molecular structure from the positions of the atoms.

Important: the exact structure type is determined by the placement of electron pairs, but the actual structure name is determined only by the positions of the atoms.

Important general rules:

  • For the VSEPR model, multiple bonds (double, triple bonds etc) count as one effective electron pair.

  • For molecules with resonance (multiple valid Lewis structures), any one of the Lewis structures can be used to predict the molecular structure using the VSEPR model.
