Chapter 6 Notes - Thermochemistry

Energy: the capacity to do work or to produce heat.

Potential energy: Energy due to position or composition.

Kinetic energy: Energy due to motion. KE = ½*m*v^2

Temperature and heat are not the same thing:

Temperature: the random motions of the particles in a particular substance.

Heat: the transfer of energy between two objects due to a temperature difference.

Work: a force acting over a distance.

System: the part of the universe on which we wish to focus attention.

Surroundings: everything else.

State Function (or State Property): a state of a system that does not depend on previous states. That is, it doesn’t matter how the system got to its current state. Eg. compression of gas in a piston. Energy, pressure and volume are all state functions, as they are simply states that don’t depend on previous states or a path.

Path Function: the opposite of a state function; a state of a system that depends on previous states, or the path between the two states. Heat and Work are both path functions, as they are energy transfers.

Thermodynamics: the study of energy and its interconversions.

The law of conservation of energy is often called the first law of thermodynamics and is stated as: “The energy of the universe is constant.”

The Internal Energy is the sum of the kinetic and potential energies of all the particles in the system. The internal energy of the system can be changed by a flow of work, heat or both. Thus: ΔE = q + w where q is heat and w is work.

The sign of thermodynamics quantities represents the direction of flow from the system’s point of view. For example, if heat is flowing into a system (endothermic), the sign of q is positive (+).

Pressure = Force / Area, so Force = Pressure * Area (P=F/A, F=P*A).

W = F*d = P*A*d = P*ΔV

In the case of gas in a cylinder, if the gas is expanding and thus the volume is increasing, the system (the gas) is exerting work on the surroundings (the piston) so the sign is negative

(W = -PΔV).

Enthalpy is the total content of heat in a system, which equals the system’s internal energy (KE+PE) plus the product of volume and pressure. It is defined as ΔH = E + PV. Since E, P, and V are all state functions, enthalpy is also a state function. Enthalpy is in units of energy, Joules (note that pressure*volume = energy). Absolute values of enthalpy cannot be determined; only changes in enthalpy.

For a chemical reaction, the enthalpy change is given by the equation:

ΔH = Hproducts - Hreactants so if the products have a higher enthalpy than the reactants, the change in enthalpy (ΔH) will be positive. Positive ΔH means an endothermic reaction, while negative enthalpy means an exothermic reaction.

Calorimetry: The science of measuring heat.

Calorimeter: A device used to experimentally determine the heat associated with a chemical reaction.

One substance might require a great deal of heat energy to raise its temperature by one degree, whereas another will exhibit the same temperature change after absorbing relatively little heat. The heat capacity C of a substance, a measure of this property, is defined as:

C = heat absorbed / increase in temperature

Since the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of an object by a certain temperature depends on how much of that object there is, measurements of substances’ heat capacities need to include a unit of mass. This is called the specific heat capacity, in units of J/(g℃) .

Constant-pressure calorimetry is the measurement of heat at a constant pressure. At constant pressure, change in enthalpy is equal to change in temperature. Thus constant-pressure calorimetry is used to determine change in enthalpy.

ΔH = ΔE + PΔV (P is const), ΔE = q + w = q - PΔV, ΔH = q - PΔV + PΔV = q => ΔH = q

(at constant pressure)

Enthalpy is a state function; the change in enthalpy from one state to another is independent of the pathway. Hess’s Law states that “in going from a particular set of reactants to a particular set of products, the change in enthalpy is the same whether the reaction takes place in one step or in a series of steps.”

Important characteristics of ΔH for a reaction:

  1. A reversed reaction has a flipped ΔH sign.

  2. The magnitude of ΔH is proportional to the quantities of reactants and products (if all coefficients are doubled, ΔH is also doubled).

The standard enthalpy of formation (ΔHf°) of a compound is defined as the change in enthalpy that accompanies the formation of 1 mole of a compound from its elements with all substances in their standard states. The degree symbol means that the corresponding process was carried out under standard conditions, which is important because thermodynamic functions often depend on the concentrations of pressures of the substances involved.

Standard States (conventional definitions):

For a Compound:

  • Gaseous substance -> 1atm

  • Pure substance in a condensed state (l/s) -> pure liquid or solid

  • Substance in a solution -> concentration of 1M

For an Element:

  • Whatever form the element exists under conditions of 1atm and 25℃

Standard enthalpies of formation (ΔHf°) can be used to calculate ΔH for a chemical reaction.

ΔH°reaction = ΣnpΔHf°(products) - ΣnpΔHf°(reactants)
